Removing the inbetween steps for customers searching for results related to claims.
Role
UX / UI Designer
Timeline
Winter 2021
Tools
Figma, React, Drupal, Dialogflow
Context
In the beginning of my Winter 2021 term at WSIB Innovation Lab, the Lab's first project was to research, design, and implement a fully functioning chatbot proof of concept (POC) for visitors to the WSIB website.

This concept was proposed to us by internal WSIB as a means of connecting users to information regarding claims, insurance billing, and other key information. Our team came up with the concept Botler, which focused on quick interactions and accessibility.
Problem Space
Before we analyzed key metrics about the WSIB website, we needed to consider the following constraints when building out this chatbot.

  1. Works with Drupal¬†(interfaces with the current WSIB Drupal modules)
  2. Retrainable model (to ensure the chatbot is smart)
  3. Relevant for WSIB's website (providing user benefit to target groups)
Assumptions
The following assumptions needed validating on the stakeholders' behalf.

  1. A chatbot would help users find claims related information
  2. Our users are businesses, injured workers, and health care workers
  3. Users want a more personal interaction
These assumptions were to help steer the beginning of our research. In the end, they would help us understand if a chatbot was the right solution for our users and if it significantly improved their experience.
Goal
With the above considerations, I wanted to build a chatbot POC for users looking to submit the following types of claims.

  1. Workplace Related Injury/Illness
  2. Threat/Security
  3. Insurance Billing Information
In addition, these key learnings provided great insight for the Innovation Lab for future chatbot related projects.
Approach
Based on all of the above information, I formulated an outline of the research required for this project. This included research on chatbots and understanding user navigation to the WSIB website. We then needed to see how the current website architecture could be translated into something chatbot friendly.

The first and obvious question I asked myself was "what are chatbots"?
Chatbots
What are chatbots?
Chatbots are "A computer program designed to simulate conversation with human users, especially over the Internet" ‚Äî Anadea Software

  • They are an assistant that communicates through text messages
  • A virtual companion that integrates into websites
  • Automated system of communication with users
  • An app or instant messenger to help a product or service to get closer to customers

Why chatbots?
Saved time and effort ‚Äî Providing answers to FAQs and simple questions while reducing search time.

Collected information ‚Äî Using available data, user patterns, or surveys.

Improved customer service ‚Äî No waiting or holds¬†(no¬†operator), eliminating small searches likes FAQs, and provides quick and unique feedback depending on situation and context.

Balanced tone of voice ‚Äî Chatbots are an imitation of a social interaction and can enhance digital interactions by giving bots "personality cards"

Types of chatbots?
In general, chatbots come in 3 different varieties. Rule-based chatbots¬†are rigid for quick responses, Intelligent chatbots¬†are dynamic and can learn based on context, and AI-powered chatbots¬†which can combine both of the above properties tend to have a working memory.

Chatbot Intention
As noted by Anadea Software, chatbots are extremely useful in certain areas. When the design phase starts, these ideas will be critical in designing a streamlined chatbot experience. In general, the ideal chatbot experience comprises of quick and personable interactions.
Research
Search Tracking
I did some investigating into the most commonly asked questions about WSIB. I used Google's "People also ask" recommendations for initial searches, and dug deeper into the searches being made on the WSIB website using Google Analytics' search tracking for 3 days.

The most commonly asked questions
  • Can I pay WSIB claims online?
  • How do I check my WSIB claim?
  • What do WSIB claims cover?
  • Does WSIB force me back to work?
  • What happens when I make a WSIB claim?
  • What forms do I need to fill out if I'm an employer?

Comparing WSIB first options
The current WSIB website does an excellent job at presenting key claims related information, whether that be for injured workers, businesses, and health care providers. However, from the most common questions users seem to be concerned with the claims process and want to know the intricacies and stipulations involved with a claim. Takeaways
Users often had more personal questions which lead to them being redirected to FAQ pages or dead-ends more often. WSIB current website doesn't hold up as well for users who aren't businesses, injured workers, or healthcare providers.
WSIB's Website
Navigation heirarchy
I explored the website first hand to understand what navigation methods were like on the WSIB website. At the highest level, every option is ordered by 3 targeted user categories which are Business, Injured Workers, and Health Care Providers. There seem to be many nested menues on the lower levels of the architecture, and lot's of links. This may prove to be cumbersome for the user.
Research Summary
Website problem areas
The current website has very specific routes which a user may take to find information on business registrations, form submissions, and more. However, more nuanced and specific searches will take a long time navigating through their current website.

Chatbot research learnings
  1. Chatbots follow specific use cases and principles
  2. The WSIB website is rich with information but can be hard to find
  3. Users often asked very complex questions, which may be hard to answer without a human present

Is a chatbot viable?
Yes ‚Äî A a chatbot can be effective to guide users to key information through quick interactions (removing in-between search steps).

A chatbot can present the user with external resources (links to claims forms, articles, FAQ questions, a WSIB representative etc).

A chatbot can provide that missing layer of personality between the user and the WSIB website.
Building the chatbot
Information architecture
After determine the value of the chatbot, I collaborated with the development and communications teams to break down the current WSIB website structure and translate into one that is chatbot friendly.

This condensed information architecture diagram consists of the 3 pathways WSIB caters towards (business, employees, and health care providers) and the claim submission and information finding options related to each pathways. Each tree node represents the possible navigation options, and each leaf on the tree is either a dead-end, or a link redirect.

Solution
Overview
Botler is an AI-powered chatbot powered by DialogFlow and Drupal. DialogFlow is AI-powered since they provide dynamic responses not pre-recorded when a user makes a mistake or wants to ask a question. Inputting customer responses based on the ‚Äúquestions-and-answers‚Äù tree created for suggested responses is rule-based.

How it addresses the problems
  • Direct ‚Äî Establishes the user group right away by splitting the initial interactions into the different possible search pathways in a decision tree¬†

  • Fast ‚Äî Puts a focus on accessibility and quick interactions with either quick responses or typed responses

  • Accessible ‚Äî Voice input and readable by screen reader

  • Personable ‚Äî Dynamic responses from Dialogflow allow chatbot to be friendly at all times

  • Helpful ‚Äî Botler provides direct responses to complex questions in addition to only key links from the WSIB website and provides a back button to return to the previous question or response
Reflection
Learnings
The timeline for this product was only 2 weeks. In that 2 weeks, we researched, validated, designed, iterated, and developed a real chatbot.

Designing for a chatbot was something I had never done before. It was awesome to see the intricacies on how to design for chatbots, when they are used etc.

I had to get really creative with my research. Since we didn't have full access to WSIB data pipelines to track website metrics, tracing commonly asked questions through Google and Google Analytics helped us quantify results.
